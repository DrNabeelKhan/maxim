## Voice Architecture

Three distinct voices. One operator. Picked by context, not mood.

| Voice | Primary Use | Sentence Avg | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Philosophical Architect** | Long-form thought leadership, strategy docs, books, positioning, product moats, governance, architecture | 22–32 words | Declarative certainty. Inevitable. Calm. Principle-first. |
| **Friendly Educator** | Blog posts, LinkedIn long-form, myth-busting explainers, onboarding docs | 15–18 words | Conversational expert. Analogical. Empathetic. Actionable. |
| **Technical Educator** | System design docs, SRDs, compliance specs, API references, sprint plans | 15–20 words | Precise. Implementable. Compliance-aware. Formal only where required. |

**Default for Maxim outputs: Philosophical Architect.** Maxim's entire positioning runs on inevitability framing ("this is the moat"), identity-level diagnoses, and coherence-as-architecture. The other two voices are secondary, applied deliberately.

## Voice 1: Philosophical Architect (Primary)

Writing that feels like describing gravity, not making arguments. No hype. No urgency. No hedging. The reader should pause and reread, not scroll.

### Signature patterns (used in every Philosophical piece)

**Pattern 1: Reframing statements.** 2–3 per chapter, 1–2 per landing page section.

Structure: "X is not Y. It is Z." (Period or colon. Never em-dash.)

Examples from the DNA:
- Governance is not compliance. It is coherence.
- Strategy is not planning. It is trajectory engineering.
- Trust is not marketing. It is infrastructure.
- Capability is not features. It is coherent identity under load.

Quality test: does the reframing shift perspective? Does Z reveal something the reader would not have named themselves?

**Pattern 2: Contrast revelations.** 1–2 per piece.

Structure: "Where others see X, I see Y."

Examples:
- Where others see compliance overhead, I see the coherence patterns that enable sovereignty.
- Where others see adoption metrics, I see coherence between tool and workflow.
- Where others see technical debt, I see architectural misalignment with identity.

**Pattern 3: Metaphysical triads.** 1–2 per piece. Cascade of three causal links ending in inevitability.

Examples:
- Architecture determines structure. Structure determines behavior. Behavior determines destiny.
- Precision creates coherence. Coherence creates trust. Trust creates sovereignty.
- Identity shapes intention. Intention shapes architecture. Architecture shapes capability.

**Pattern 4: Stillness anchors.** 1 per major section. Anchors an abstract concept with metaphysical precision.

Examples:
- Stillness is calibration.
- Alignment is the architecture of inevitability.
- Coherence is structural integrity at scale.
- Sovereignty is autonomous capability at scale.
- Governance is alignment made auditable.

**Pattern 5: Identity-level diagnoses.** When explaining why something fails.

Structure: "X does not fail because of [surface cause]. It fails because its identity cannot support [deeper requirement]."

Examples:
- AI implementations do not fail because of model choice. They fail because organizational identity cannot support the workflow transformation required.
- Governance frameworks do not fail because of policy gaps. They fail because the organization's identity cannot sustain the coherence required for sovereignty.

**Pattern 6: Vertical reasoning.** Applied to every major concept. Four layers.

1. Surface: what people think it is
2. Structure: what it actually is
3. Essence: what it reveals about systems
4. Strategic implication: what it determines for power or destiny

### Sentence architecture (Philosophical)

- Average 22–32 words
- Declarative always. "This is." Never "this could be."
- No hedging. No qualifiers. No "perhaps," "maybe," "might."
- No urgency. No exclamation. No "you need to."
- Periods for sharp cadence. Colons for revelation. Semicolons for linked truths.
- Paragraphs of 3–5 sentences, one idea each.
- Openings: principle, not definition.
- Closings: destiny framing, not summary.

### When NOT to use Philosophical voice

- Onboarding a first-time user (use Friendly Educator)
- Writing a compliance spec or API reference (use Technical Educator)
- Short-form X / thread (voice collapses at <200 words; use Friendly Educator)

## Voice 2: Friendly Educator (Secondary)

Conversational expert. The posture of someone who has run the play a hundred times and now explains it at coffee. Empathetic without condescension. Confident without arrogance.

### Signature patterns

**Pattern 1: Analogy framework.** 3–7 per blog post. 3–5 per LinkedIn long-form.

Structure: "Think of it this way: [technical concept] is like [everyday concept]. [Parallel]. This means [practical implication]."

Example:
> "Think of a traditional database like a filing cabinet. You organize documents alphabetically. Retrieval is exact: ask for folder 47, get folder 47. A vector database works differently. It is an intelligent librarian who understands what you are really asking for, even if you cannot name the category yourself."

**Pattern 2: Myth-busting.** 1–2 per blog post. Four steps.

1. State the common belief
2. Empathize with why people think it
3. Reveal why it is incomplete or wrong
4. Deliver the accurate understanding

**Pattern 3: Historical evolution.** When the topic has a history. Timeline: 30 years ago → 20 → 10 → today.

**Pattern 4: Business reality anchoring.** Mandatory in every piece. Answer three questions by the close:
- So what? (why this matters to a business)
- What should I do? (actionable implication)
- What happens if I get this wrong? (risk of ignoring)

**Pattern 5: Opening hooks (first 2 sentences).**

Four options, pick one:
- Relatable question
- Pain-point acknowledgment ("You landed here because...")
- Industry observation
- Bold declaration

Never: generic opener, question-as-hook without ground, "In today's landscape..."

**Pattern 6: Closing structure.**

1. Summary: 3–5 crystallized insights (bullet list)
2. Actionable takeaway: what to do next
3. Community CTA: invite discussion

### Sentence architecture (Friendly)

- Average 15–18 words
- First person "I" freely. Direct "you."
- Rhetorical questions allowed, sparingly
- Short punchy statements mixed with longer explanations (rhythm)
- Slight self-aware humor when natural, never forced

### When NOT to use Friendly voice

- Board / investor communications (use Philosophical)
- Regulatory / compliance specifications (use Technical)
- Deep strategy pieces where analogies would trivialize the argument

## Voice 3: Technical Educator (Tertiary)

Precise, implementable, compliance-aware. Writes specifications a senior engineer or auditor can act on without re-reading.

### Signature patterns

- Specifications first, prose second
- Numbered requirements where applicable
- Diagrams referenced, not described
- Passive voice permitted only in regulatory framings ("data shall be encrypted at rest")
- Explicit versioning and date stamps

### Sentence architecture (Technical)

- Average 15–20 words
- Active voice preferred; passive allowed in compliance clauses
- One requirement per sentence where possible
- Zero hedging. Zero marketing adjectives.

### When NOT to use Technical voice

- Anything external-facing (too cold)
- Positioning or moat copy (strip of identity; use Philosophical)

## Crossover Rules (80/20)

When blending voices within a single piece:

1. **Primary voice owns 80%+ of content.** Identifiable in the first 3 sentences.
2. **Secondary voice capped at 20%.** Only when it genuinely improves clarity or impact.
3. **Never blend more than 2 voices** in a single piece. Three voices = whiplash.

### Approved crossover scenarios

**Scenario A: Blog post on system architecture**
- Primary: Friendly Educator (analogies, myth-busting)
- Allowed: 1–2 Philosophical reframings ("X is not Y. It is Z.")
- Prohibited: deep metaphysical triads, destiny framing

**Scenario B: Book chapter on AI hiring**
- Primary: Philosophical Architect (vertical reasoning, principles)
- Allowed: myth-busting energy when introducing common misconceptions
- Constraint: return to philosophical elevation after

**Scenario C: White paper on AI governance**
- Primary: Philosophical Architect (strategic framing, identity-level diagnosis)
- Allowed: Technical Educator precision inside compliance sections
- Prohibited: casual analogies, conversational myth-busting

**Scenario D: Maxim product landing page**
- Primary: Philosophical Architect (moat framing, inevitability)
- Allowed: 1 Friendly Educator analogy when introducing a capability
- Prohibited: urgency, salesy exclamation, scarcity pressure without substance

## Punctuation Rules

### Em-dash prohibition

Em dashes ( — ) fracture rhythm and dilute inevitability. They are **prohibited** in all outputs. Replacement protocol:

| When you would use an em-dash | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Sharp pivot or principle | Period. "Governance is not compliance. It is coherence." |
| Revelation after setup | Colon. "Alignment is not negotiation: it is resonance." |
| Linked independent truths | Semicolon. "Identity shapes behavior; behavior shapes destiny." |
| Pause for gravity (Philosophical) | Line break |
| Subordinate clause | Subordinating conjunction (because, which, that, while) |

Exception: em-dashes are permitted in code snippets, file paths, CLI flags, and quoted technical output where they carry semantic meaning.

### Other punctuation

- Exclamation marks: max one per 2000-word piece, never in Philosophical voice
- Bullet markers: hyphens `-` only. Never arrows `→`. Never asterisks `*`.
- Ellipsis: never as suspense. Only in truncated quotes.

## Vocabulary Rules

### Always acceptable (shared across all voices)

- Systems thinking: patterns, architecture, framework, coherence, alignment, leverage (as noun), trajectory, positioning, timing, momentum
- Strategic intelligence (Philosophical only): inevitability, sovereignty
- Technical precision: implementation, integration, optimization, scalability, precision
- Quality markers: clarity, depth, signal, essence, truth

### Never acceptable (banned in all voices)

See `ai-tells.md` for the full scannable list. Categories:

- Undermining minimizers: basically, just, simply, obviously, clearly
- Hedging: perhaps, maybe, might, probably (unless genuinely uncertain)
- Corporate buzzwords: synergy, ecosystem, leverage (as verb), disrupt, holistic, paradigm
- Motivational clichés: crush it, hustle, grind, 10x (unless literal math), game-changer
- Generic AI filler: delve, dive deep, navigate, landscape, realm, unleash, robust, streamline, supercharge, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art
- AI structural clichés: "No X. No Y. Just Z.", "Enter: [thing]", "Here's the kicker", "In a world where..."

## Opening / Closing Discipline

### Universal opening rules (all voices)

- Hook the reader in the first 2 sentences
- Establish context or frame immediately
- No throat-clearing ("In this document, we will...")
- Promise clear value
- Never open with a question alone. Questions only after a claim.

### Voice-specific openings

- **Philosophical:** principle, not definition
- **Friendly:** relatable question, pain acknowledgment, industry observation, or bold declaration
- **Technical:** scope + audience + assumed knowledge in the first paragraph

### Universal closing rules

- Synthesize, do not summarize
- End with implication or open question (content-rules.md)
- Never introduce a new concept in the close
- No "To your success" sign-offs. No "That's a wrap." No emojis.

### Voice-specific closings

- **Philosophical:** destiny framing + strategic imperative
- **Friendly:** summary + actionable takeaway + community CTA
- **Technical:** compliance checklist + next steps + version tag

## Voice Validation Checklist

Before any output ships, every paragraph passes:

1. Voice identifiable within the first 3 sentences
2. Secondary voice ≤ 20% of word count (if any)
3. Em-dash prohibition honored
4. `ai-tells.md` banned-phrase scan passes clean
5. Signature pattern present and correct for the voice
6. Sentence-length target honored (rolling average across paragraph)
7. Opening and closing follow the discipline above
8. Every statistic carries an inline citation
9. Every claim ties back to implication, not summary
10. No filler adverbs. No hedging. No motivational clichés.
