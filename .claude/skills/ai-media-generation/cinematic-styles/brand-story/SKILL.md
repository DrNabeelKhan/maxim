# Maxim Brand Story — Commitment Engine via Narrative

## The Maxim Moat

Other libraries teach narrative brand film craft — founder stories, origin arcs, mission videos. Pretty brand films with brand-as-hero mistake that underperforms.

Maxim's brand-story style is engineered from **StoryBrand + Hero's Journey first, narrative craft second**. The customer is ALWAYS the hero; the brand is ALWAYS the guide. This single principle, rigorously applied, is the difference between brand films that build commitment and those that read as self-absorbed marketing.

## Primary Behavioral Framework

### StoryBrand (Donald Miller, 2017)

The customer is the hero of the story. The brand is the guide. This inverts the most common brand-story mistake (brand-as-hero = viewer alienation).

**Seven-element structure:**
1. A **character** (customer) has a problem
2. They meet a **guide** (brand)
3. Guide gives them a **plan**
4. That calls them to **action**
5. Which helps them **avoid failure**
6. And **succeed** (resolution)
7. Creating a **transformation**

**Why it works:**
- Viewer identifies with customer (hero) not brand
- Brand earns trust through guide role (competent + caring)
- Transformation arc creates emotional investment
- Viewer leaves with "I see myself in this" rather than "that brand is nice"

### Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell, 1949)

The mythological narrative structure (Ordinary World → Call → Trials → Transformation → Return). Maps to the extended brand story when StoryBrand's 7-element structure is deepened for longer formats.

**Application:**
- StoryBrand is skeletal (short form)
- Hero's Journey is muscular (long form, 2+ minutes)
- Together they cover brand story of any length

### Combined mechanism

StoryBrand ensures the correct perspective (customer-as-hero, brand-as-guide). Hero's Journey provides depth for longer format. Result: viewer identifies emotionally + sees brand as catalyst-for-change + commits to ongoing relationship.

## Behavioral → Cinematic Translation

| Narrative choice | Behavioral mechanism activated |
|---|---|
| Customer protagonist (not brand) | StoryBrand correct perspective |
| Brand appears at "guide" moment (mid-story) | StoryBrand role definition |
| Protagonist emotional close-up | Mirror Neurons + Narrative Transportation |
| Voiceover first-person / past-tense | Narrative Transportation activation |
| Trial montage (3-5 failed attempts) | Hero's Journey trials phase |
| Guide appears quietly (not dramatically) | StoryBrand trust signal (guide = competent not flashy) |
| Peak at 70-80% runtime | Peak-End Rule peak engineering |
| Resonant final image | Peak-End Rule end |
| Brand wordmark as subtle signature | Reinforces guide-not-hero positioning |

## The 5-Act Structure (StoryBrand + Hero's Journey synthesis)

### Act 1 — Ordinary World / Character (0-15%) — activates Mirror Neurons + Narrative Transportation
Introduce protagonist (customer or customer-avatar) in pre-transformation state. Viewer begins identification.

### Act 2 — Call + Refusal / Problem (15-30%) — activates StoryBrand problem
Inciting incident. Protagonist faces specific problem. Pain is visible. Viewer empathy deepens.

### Act 3 — Trials + Guide Appears / Plan (30-65%) — activates StoryBrand guide role + Hero's Journey trials
Protagonist tries and fails. Brand appears quietly as guide. Brand gives them a plan (product/service as path).

### Act 4 — Transformation Peak (65-85%) — activates Peak-End Rule peak
Protagonist succeeds with brand's help. Peak emotional moment. Widest shot, warmest grade, slowest motion.

### Act 5 — New Normal + Call to Viewer (85-100%) — activates behavioral transfer
Protagonist's transformed state. Brief, affirming. Brand signature. Viewer prompted to begin their own story.

## Sales / Lead-Gen Templates

### Template 1 — Customer-hero brand story 2:30 (optimizing for commitment + purchase intent)

```
Customer-hero brand story, 2:30 runtime, documentary aesthetic, StoryBrand
structure depth.

ACT 1 — Character (0:00-0:20):
Meet [CUSTOMER] in their world. Their struggle. Their unfulfilled need.
Observational, empathetic camera. Natural light. Verité aesthetic.
Voiceover: "I spent [X] years thinking [FALSE BELIEF]. I didn't know
there was another way." Past tense, first-person.
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: Customer-as-hero established (StoryBrand). Narrative
Transportation onset. Viewer mirrors protagonist.

ACT 2 — Problem (0:20-0:50):
[CUSTOMER] encounters problem at its worst. Close-up emotional moment at
0:40. Viewer empathy peaks. Voiceover: "Then one day, [SPECIFIC TRIGGER]."
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: StoryBrand problem crystalization. Emotional Arc
"Man-in-a-Hole" low point.

ACT 3 — Guide Appears + Plan (0:50-1:30):
Brand enters story quietly — not dramatically. Not savior. Competent and
caring guide. Shows [CUSTOMER] a plan — specific steps, specific tool
([PRODUCT]). Brand footage naturally integrated. Trust-building, not
selling.
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: Brand role clarified. Trust transfer begins. Plan
gives customer direction = Hero's Journey "mentor" beat.

ACT 4 — Action + Trials (1:30-1:50):
[CUSTOMER] begins using [PRODUCT/SERVICE] following brand's plan. Not
instant success — realistic trials. Building momentum. Progress visible.
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: Hero's Journey trials phase sustains engagement.
Realistic depiction builds credibility.

ACT 5 — Victory + Peak (1:50-2:15):
[CUSTOMER] succeeds. Life changed concretely (shown, not told). Emotional
peak at 2:05 — widest shot of film, warmest grade, slow-motion on customer
expression moment. Brand visible but background.
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: Peak-End Rule peak engineering. Catharsis. Memory
encoding at maximum.

ACT 6 — Call to Viewer (2:15-2:30):
[CUSTOMER] speaks to camera: "If you're where I was, here's where you
start." Brand signature appears simply. CTA. Final frame holds 3 seconds.
BEHAVIORAL FUNCTION: StoryBrand call-to-action. Viewer prompted to begin
their own journey — now identifies as a "customer-hero in progress."
```

### Template 2 — Founder origin brand film 2:00 (brand-identity version of 01-cinematic Template 1 but with StoryBrand applied)

Note: when founder IS the hero, this is a valid variation. Customer-hero remains primary pattern.

```
Founder origin with StoryBrand wrinkle (customer parallel), 2:00, dual-track.

The story interleaves founder's origin with a typical customer's parallel
journey — both arrive at the same "using this brand changed things" moment.
This gives customer-as-hero pattern even when founder is the narrative spine.

Acts as per template 1 but footage alternates:
  - 40% founder's arc
  - 40% customer's parallel arc
  - 20% brand-as-guide footage
Final peak unites the two arcs at the moment brand connects founder's
solution to customer's problem.
```

### Template 3 — Mission-driven anthem 90s (Act structure compressed)

```
Mission anthem 90s, Emotional Arc = Man-in-a-Hole, StoryBrand macro-structure.

ACT 1 (0-15s): character reality — the problem the brand exists to solve
shown via rapid montage of real customers/communities affected.
ACT 2 (15-35s): what if — counter-possibility. Brand concept as "plan."
ACT 3 (35-60s): brand in action — montage of impact, real customers
benefiting.
ACT 4 (60-75s): peak — single resonant image of vision realized.
ACT 5 (75-90s): join us CTA.
```

## Anti-Patterns (Commitment Killers)

| Anti-pattern | Breaks |
|---|---|
| Brand-as-hero | Kills StoryBrand — most common mistake |
| Generic founder-working-late montage | Breaks Narrative Transportation (viewer checks out) |
| Mission statement as spoken dialogue | Breaks show-don't-tell |
| Even pacing (no peak) | Breaks Peak-End Rule |
| Over-length without payoff | Breaks Completion Rate |
| Brand appearing at beginning as savior | Breaks StoryBrand guide role |
| Happy-customer voice-over scripted | Breaks authenticity — viewer detects |
| Ignoring .brand-foundation | Voice conflict with established brand character |
| Abstract mission without customer connection | Breaks StoryBrand — hero missing |
